I'm currently reading Ann Radcliffe's novel [The Romance of the Forest](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Romance-Forest-Gothic-Novel/dp/0979729041), written in 1791. I felt like diving into the classics of English Gothic fiction.

At first it was hard going, but now I can't put it down, even though I'm still reading rather slowly.

The plot is about the unfortunate orphan Adeline who, fleeing from danger, finds herself in a remote forest, among the ruins of an old abbey…

Imagine a night: outside the window a storm is roaring, thunder shakes the walls. Adeline notices that the tapestry in her room is moving, and behind it there is a secret door. Curiosity overcomes fear, and she steps into the darkness, where she finds a rusty dagger and an old manuscript. With trembling hands she unfolds the papers, trying to read a dreadful secret of the past… and at that very moment her lamp goes out. Darkness. Silence. And someone's footsteps. 🕯️😱

Eighteenth-century English is very difficult - lots of unfamiliar words, sentences are built differently - you really have to recalibrate your "reading optics".

It's hard to fight your way through archaisms like _hither_ (here), _yonder_ (over there), or _heinous_ (horrible), as well as _'tis_, _thy_, _o'er_… 😑

I've always liked discovering linguistic curiosities, and there are plenty of them here, for example:

- The verb **"suffer"** used in the sense of **"let"** - for example _"suffer me to go"_. I'm stealing that one.

- **"features"** in an old-fashioned sense: _"forcing a smile upon her features"_. Today English speakers wouldn't talk about facial features here; they'd just say _"she forced a smile"_.

- _"what mean you, sir"_ - who talks like that? Today it would be _"what **DO** you mean, sir?"_

- _"At supper the Marquis was less gay than usual"_ - wait, less gay than usual? 😁  
  Of course, _gay_ used to mean something like _cheerful_, so it's: "At supper the Marquis was less cheerful than usual."

- _"yet it was long ere"_ - **ERE??** Ah, so: _yet it was long before_.

And so on…

But the most interesting one:

**"You was speaking of the Marquis…"**

Not _you were_, but _you was_!

This is not a mistake. In earlier English there was a distinction between informal _thou_ and formal _you_, not just a universal _you_ as today.

Words like _thou_ were used for close or intimate address, while _you_ or _ye_ were used for formal or plural address.

Thou - you (singular)  
Thee - you (object)  
Thy - your  
Thine - your (before a vowel)

Ye - you (plural)

How about this:

- _thou art_ (you are)
- _thou hast_ (you have) - basically [Thou, thou hast, thou hast mich](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W3q8Od5qJio) 😆
- _thou dost_ (you do)
- _thou speakest_ (you speak)

Later, _you_ pushed out _thou_, _ye_, and _thee_, and English lost the distinction between informal and formal "you".

Everyone is just _you_.  
Your boss - _you_.  
Your dog - _you_.  
The Queen - _you_.

Many people who have lived in Britain for decades don't even realize that modern English addresses everyone using only this single "you".

What's interesting is that this replacement seems to have happened across all major English-speaking countries - from America to Australia - even though these countries were discovered and colonized at a time when _thou_ was still actively used 🤔
